Naxi schools (2004)

 

Back from Fengke, I presented my research at a continuing-education meeting of school teachers from the whole Lijiang District. All of them speakers of Naxi, they were curious to hear a foreigner present (in Naxi) his research on their language. (I remain in touch with some of them.) On the basis of mutual respect and curiosity, the discussion gets started easily. Their first questions are: Why stay in several places rather than acquire a good proficiency in the "standard" variety of Naxi? Why not start out from the study of the written language? (Naxi has a pictographic script) Why do research on language varieties without a literary tradition? Why record and transcribe complex narratives (craftsmen's explanations containing specialised vocabulary, folk tales containing archaic formulations) when one still expresses oneself in a clumsy way, using a child-like vocabulary? The gap to bridge is in fact the same as with the informants (now referred to as language consultants, to bring out the fact that they pass on to the linguist pieces of knowledge that he did not possess). The complicity which develops in the course of the exchange is all the more precious for having been gained step by step.

 

The meeting of school teachers. Can you spot the visitor?

A teacher of the Fengke high school, and the person in charge of the local Bureau of Education, Mr. Guang Li. During my stay in Fengke, I spent one day teaching English classes in the nearest high school (2 hrs' walk from the place where I stayed).

 

An inscription on the wall of a school, in the mountains, one day's drive from Lijiang: "Please speak Mandarin". Even here, teachers and parents encourage the children to speak Mandarin Chinese, the language of education; Mandarin is also the language of television (warmly welcome by the villagers), which slowly effaces linguistics frontiers. For the linguist, it is urgent to describe the considerable variety of Naxi dialects, which is the depository of this language's history.

 

Since 2004, I have had more opportunities to exchange with Naxi colleagues. A major priority locally is training in the International Phonetic Alphabet, so that colleagues who are interested in collecting data have adequate tools to transcribe unfamiliar sounds when they come across them. There is also a major language gap, as publications in English are still intimidating to many colleagues over here, so I do my best to make available in Chinese recent findings that may be useful to Chinese colleagues.
